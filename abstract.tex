\begin{abstract}
This thesis investigates the design, implementation, and evaluation of a \ac{LKA} function inside a software-only \ac{VIL} simulation environment based on the CARLA simulator. The work develops a complete control stack that runs as an external Python process, communicates with the simulator through its synchronous-mode Python API at $20$~Hz, and exercises the same vehicle, the same map, and the same target speed across three progressively richer lateral-control architectures: a cross-track PID closed on the perpendicular distance to a pre-recorded reference path, a heading-error PI closed on the angle to a speed-dependent look-ahead point on the same reference, and a vision-based PI that replaces the pre-recorded reference with a center line reconstructed at every tick from the RGB camera by a deep convolutional lane detector and by an inverse-perspective-mapping projection.

The vehicle is modelled as a dynamic single-track with parameters read from the CARLA blueprint, and a complete system-identification procedure fits the longitudinal time constant and the lateral steering gain from two open-loop step experiments performed in the simulator. The resulting plant parameters are used to derive analytical PID gains by pole placement, with explicit gain-scheduling expressions that show that the cross-track gains scale as $1/v^2$ while the heading-error gains scale as $1/v$---a structural advantage of the heading-error formulation that is reflected in a larger closed-loop phase margin and in a gentler dependence on the vehicle speed.

The three architectures are evaluated on a common test route in Town06 with two scalar metrics: the \ac{RMSE} and the maximum absolute cross-track error. The heading-error PI achieves the best performance with an \ac{RMSE} of $0.120$~m and a maximum excursion of $1.185$~m, against $0.205$~m and $2.217$~m of the cross-track PID---an improvement by a factor of approximately $1.7$ on the average and $1.9$ on the worst case, consistent with the control-theoretic predictions on the reduced plant order. The vision-based PI achieves an \ac{RMSE} of $0.270$~m, dominated by a steady-state bias of approximately $0.7$~m on smooth curves that is traced back to specific approximations of the perception layer. On a step-like lane discontinuity, the vision-based controller is shown to produce the smoothest physical trajectory at the cost of a large \emph{apparent} error against the discontinuous path-based reference.

The thesis contributes a modular, reproducible software-only \ac{VIL} implementation of an \ac{LKA} function, a complete system-identification and analytical tuning pipeline, a side-by-side control-theoretic and experimental comparison of three lateral-control architectures, and an explicit junction-handling logic that makes the vision-based controller usable on routes that include intersections. The closing chapter outlines a structured set of short-, medium-, and long-term extensions, ranging from a curvature-aware look-ahead correction and a model-predictive controller, through sensor fusion between camera and map, to real-vehicle validation and learning-based control policies.
\end{abstract}
